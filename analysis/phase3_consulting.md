# Phase 3 - Consulting Industry: AI Penetration and Entry-Point Assessment

Scope: strategy consulting (MBB), management & advisory (Big 4 / Accenture / Hackett etc.), IT/AI implementation consultants, boutique strategy houses, independent consultants, freelance consultants, and fractional executives. The consulting industry is uniquely heterogeneous - the same job title spans a 22-year-old McKinsey BA writing slides and a $1,500/day ex-McKinsey Partner running a solo Substack-funded practice. This report treats those as separate sub-segments because they map to wildly different product opportunities.

---

## A. AI Penetration Maturity

**Headline: 8/10 - one of the most AI-saturated white-collar industries on earth, but with a sharp internal split between standardized tool adoption (top firms) and DIY-stack adoption (independents).**

### A.1 Big firms have already industrialized AI inside core workflow
Every major firm has shipped a proprietary, branded, GPT-4-class internal assistant on essentially the same 2023-2024 timeline: McKinsey Lilli, BCG Deckster, Bain Sage, Deloitte PairD, PwC ChatPwC, EY EYQ, KPMG Workbench, plus Accenture's massive Microsoft-Copilot deployment [P3CON-004]. This is not a pilot - it is the standard. At McKinsey, more than 75% of 43,000 employees use Lilli monthly, with ~500,000 prompts/month and ~17 weekly launches per user [P3CON-002]. BCG reports ~40% of associates use Deckster weekly [P3CON-024]. Internal time-and-motion studies suggest each Lilli session saves ~6 minutes of document hunting, redeploying ~50,000 consultant hours and ~$12M/month of fully-loaded labor [P3CON-003].

The work being absorbed is the entire research-to-slide pipeline that historically defined the "junior consultant" job: summarizing documents, building first-draft decks, running comparable-company analyses, checking logic, drafting interview guides. McKinsey's "One-Click" PowerPoint agent now ingests a short prompt and produces a client-ready deck against approved templates, with a Tone-of-Voice checker that rewrites text into McKinsey house style [P3CON-023]. This is the most striking AI penetration of any industry studied: AI now produces the actual deliverable artifact (the deck) end-to-end at the most prestigious firm in the sector.

### A.2 AI is also the dominant revenue line
BCG: 25% of $14.4B 2025 revenue (~$3.6B) is AI consulting work [P3CON-012]. McKinsey: 40% of client work has an AI component [P3CON-012]. BCG hired 1,000 dedicated AI staff in 2024 alone [P3CON-001]; Accenture targets 80,000 data/AI professionals by 2026 [P3CON-001]. So AI is simultaneously the productivity tool, the service offering, and the hiring priority.

### A.3 The labor consequence is real
McKinsey cut ~200 tech/support staff in late 2025 and signaled additional non-client reductions over two years; Bain, Deloitte, KPMG, Accenture all executed 2025 layoffs; PwC and EY deferred MBA start dates [P3CON-009]. The HBR claim is that AI is dismantling the historical "large junior cohort -> high attrition -> few partners" model [P3CON-008]. By 2025, AI agent roles outnumbered entry-level consultant positions at McKinsey [P3CON-009]. Junior leverage ratios are compressing.

### A.4 Independent / fractional segment - AI adoption is high but stack is fragmented
Solo and boutique consultants are equally AI-saturated but on a DIY public-tool stack (Claude, GPT, Perplexity, Fathom, Gamma) [P3CON-005]. The economics shift sharply: a 48-hour competitive analysis that took four weeks manually now sells for $1,500-$3,000, giving effective hourly rates of $200-$500 [P3CON-005]. Boutiques are growing 2-3x faster than MBB, some 100-200% YoY, partly because AI eliminates much of the scale advantage of large firms [P3CON-019]. *Inference (not fact)*: indie AI penetration is probably higher than at any large firm, because indies have no procurement friction.

### A.5 Where AI is NOT yet replacing consultants
Client-facing senior consultants are not being cut at scale; relationship-based work, accountability, judgment in messy client context, and change management with humans remain stubbornly human [P3CON-013]. The pyramid is flattening at the bottom, not collapsing at the top.

**The AI inflection point in consulting has already passed.** Unlike (say) healthcare or legal, the question is no longer "will it land" but "how does career identity work when AI ate the artifact that defined junior status."

## B. What "AI Capability" Means in This Industry

### B.1 Required, not preferred
AI proficiency has crossed from preferred to mandatory at the hiring gate. Bloomberg: MBAs seeking consulting jobs must be AI-proficient [P3CON-011]. McKinsey began piloting a final-round interview in late 2025 in which the candidate uses Lilli live to solve a business problem - prompting, evaluating output, refining, synthesizing - with the interviewer watching how the candidate collaborates with the model [P3CON-010]. AI implementation cases now appear in ~1 of 3 first-round MBB interviews [P3CON-010]. AI literacy is the new "consulting math."

### B.2 Definition splits into three layers
1. **Tool fluency**: ability to drive Lilli/Deckster/ChatGPT/Claude for research synthesis, deck drafting, comp analysis, modeling. This is now table stakes [P3CON-010].
2. **Workflow design / prompt engineering**: building reusable agents and prompt patterns - e.g. interview-guide generators, market-sizing skeletons, due-diligence checklists [P3CON-005].
3. **Strategic AI advisory**: ability to advise a client on AI strategy, build an AI-roadmap case, evaluate use-case ROI, set up governance. BCG, McKinsey, Bain all hire specifically for this; demanded skills include "AI strategy & value identification, MLOps, applied GenAI/prompt design, change management for AI adoption, AI governance and risk" [P3CON-012].

### B.3 How firms evaluate it
- **Live AI-collaboration exercises** in interviews [P3CON-010]
- **Portfolio of AI implementation projects** for laterals
- **MBB "T-shaped consultant" model**: broad business + deep vertical (AI, digital, sector) [P3CON-012]
- **BCG is actively limiting "generalist MBA" hires** in favor of engineering / data science / AI talent [P3CON-012]
- The compensation signal: AI-expertise consultants earn ~10% premium over peers [P3CON-012]; AI consultant day rates command 20-30% over baseline for GenAI specialists [P3CON-018]

### B.4 What JDs say (extracted from search aggregates)
The Axial Search analysis of 1,859 AI strategy postings (Nov 2024 - Jan 2025): 71% target 10+ years experience; median salary $221K; middle 80% $147K-$310K. Top hiring industries: Tech 31%, Professional Services (i.e. consulting itself) 21%, Financial Services 15% [via WebSearch result]. Translation: "AI strategist" is increasingly a *mid-senior* role, not entry-level, mirroring the talent-pyramid flattening.

### B.5 Key tension
The thing being assessed - "can you fruitfully collaborate with AI on a strategic problem" - is **not yet well captured by any credential, certificate, or resume line**. It is a performance, not a possession. This is the structural opening for an evidence-based identity product.

## C. User Behavior - How Consultants Display Capability

### C.1 The fundamental constraint: deliverables are confidential
A consultant's actual product - the client deck, the financial model, the change-management plan - is almost always under NDA. Standard portfolio tactics in design (show the artifact) do not work in consulting. The NDA literature suggests: anonymize logos/brands/colors, focus on process over product, build generic redacted case studies, supplement with personal projects [P3CON-015]. But "process narratives without the artifact" is exactly what LinkedIn already does poorly. **This NDA constraint is the structural reason a consultant's online identity is so weakly differentiated** - everyone says the same thing because the only thing they can legally say is the same thing.

### C.2 Where do consultants show up online
- **LinkedIn**: dominant. ~80% of one independent consultant's 2024 clients came via LinkedIn [P3CON-016]. 73% of fractional executives secure clients there; 92.8% of fractional pros say they win clients through referrals + networking [P3CON-017]. LinkedIn is the de facto "consultant resume."
- **Personal websites**: complement, not substitute. House case studies (sanitized), pricing, booking funnels [P3CON-016]. Most independents use Squarespace / Wix / Webflow templates - low differentiation.
- **Substack newsletters / thought-leadership blogs**: increasingly important - the Lenny Rachitsky / Jason Liu model. Jason Liu, an AI consultant, closes five-figure deals and maintains a waitlist; he teaches a Maven course on indie AI consulting [P3CON-020]. Substack thought leadership is becoming the primary asset for "alpha" indies.
- **Marketplaces**: Catalant ($3.2B consulting-marketplace sector, ~40% of independent deals), Toptal, COMATCH, Malt, Consultport, Business Talent Group [P3CON-021]. Used as supplementary pipeline, rarely as primary identity.

### C.3 The credibility stack for indies
"Identity without evidence is a claim. Credibility requires proof that has been documented, published, and indexed." [P3CON-014]. The new evidence stack for indies is: quantified outcomes, named case studies, published frameworks, video testimonials, podcast appearances, framework repositories. Plus a new emerging signal: **AI visibility** - can ChatGPT/Perplexity/Google AI Overviews find and recommend you when a buyer asks "best fractional CMO for Series-B SaaS in EU healthtech?" [P3CON-014]. This re-indexes consultant identity from human-network discovery to AI-mediated discovery.

### C.4 The fractional segment is the inflection point
- LinkedIn profiles mentioning "fractional" grew from 2,000 (2022) to 110,000 (early 2024) - a **55x increase in two years** [P3CON-006]
- Fractional marketing leaders: 60K -> 120K (2022-2024), 100% growth [P3CON-006]
- Demand for fractional leaders +68% YoY [P3CON-006]
- 25% of US businesses use fractional hiring, projected 35% by 2025 [P3CON-006]
- Global fractional market $5.7B (2024) -> $19.1B (2033 projected), 14.2% CAGR [P3CON-006]

These are people who have **already left** traditional employment and **already need** to manage a personal brand as their pipeline. The brand pressure is acute: "'Fractional CMO' is generic. 'Fractional CMO for B2B SaaS scaling $2M-$10M ARR' is memorable" [P3CON-017]. They are also under-served by LinkedIn for the obvious reason: LinkedIn's economic interest is employer-to-employee, not independent-to-clients.

### C.5 Pain points (synthesized)
1. **NDA cage**: cannot show the actual work [P3CON-015]
2. **Identity homogeneity**: LinkedIn profiles look interchangeable; thousands of "fractional CMOs" with identical headlines [P3CON-017]
3. **Inbound dependence**: 92.8% of fractionals get clients via referrals/networking - pipeline is brittle [P3CON-017]
4. **Pricing legibility**: clients cannot easily compare; pricing is opaque, day-rates range 5x [P3CON-018]
5. **AI-visibility anxiety**: a new fear of being invisible to AI search [P3CON-014]
6. **Inside large firms**: junior consultants experience identity crisis - the deck they spent 14 hours on is now produced in 14 minutes by Lilli, and they don't know what they own [P3CON-002 + P3CON-009]

## D. Entry-Point Evaluation

### D.1 User reachability
- **High** for fractionals/indies - they cluster on LinkedIn, Substack, X, Maven, podcasts (Lenny's, How I Built This style). They actively seek tools that grow their pipeline.
- **Medium** for boutique consultants - present on LinkedIn but more employer-mediated.
- **Low** for MBB/Big 4 staff consultants - they are inside firms with strict comms and IP controls; AI identity is firm-controlled.

### D.2 Willingness to pay / share
- **High** for fractionals & indies - they already pay for Maven cohorts, LinkedIn Premium, Hampton, RGA, Pavilion communities, brand-building agencies; Jason Liu's indie AI consulting cohort is paid [P3CON-020]. They view brand as a P&L investment, not a vanity spend.
- Willingness to **share data** is the key asymmetry: fractionals *want* their professional information indexed and findable. They will provide it voluntarily and update it actively - which directly serves our B-side data thesis.
- **Medium** for boutique consultants
- **Low** for MBB staff consultants (firm controls communications)

### D.3 Market size
- US: ~840K-1M management consultants [P3CON-022]; global ~3M [P3CON-022]
- Of these, the relevant slice: ~100K+ fractional executives [P3CON-007]; LinkedIn-self-identified "fractional" profiles ~110K (early 2024), growing 55x in two years [P3CON-006]
- Plus the indie segment: an unknown but large fraction of the ~3M global consultants who operate independently or in firms <20 people. Conservatively 300K-600K globally.
- Plus an emerging "ex-MBB / ex-FAANG turning indie AI consultant" segment - small in absolute terms but high-LTV ($800-$1,500+/day) [P3CON-018]. These are the trend-setters / aspirational users.

### D.4 Why consulting fits our product thesis (the core argument)
Consulting is unusually well-aligned with the "AI-era career identity" thesis for four reasons:

1. **The artifact problem is structural and durable**. NDAs are not going away; AI doesn't fix them. The "work portfolio" model that works for designers, devs, marketers literally cannot work for consultants without an intermediating layer. *Whoever builds the canonical sanitized-case-study + outcome-claim infrastructure for consultants captures a defensible position.*

2. **The fractional/indie wave is structurally pro-personal-brand**. They have already chosen brand-as-pipeline as their business model [P3CON-017]. Our product is selling air to people inside a vacuum.

3. **AI is collapsing the credential moat**. McKinsey-trained consultants no longer need McKinsey infrastructure to deliver McKinsey-quality work, so the prestige-firm signal is dropping (slowly) in value [P3CON-019]. New evidence-based signals (case-study libraries, framework repositories, AI-visibility scores) are filling the vacuum [P3CON-014]. *Inference (not fact)*: this is creating an ~5-year window before incumbent platforms (LinkedIn, Catalant) lock down the new identity layer.

4. **B-side data is naturally rich**. Consultants self-describe with industry vertical, functional expertise, deal size, geography, language - exactly the fine-grained tags that B-side recruiters/hirers pay for. Plus the AI-skill overlay (which agent stacks you've built, which AI implementations you've shipped) is high-signal for a particular kind of buyer.

### D.5 Risks and counter-arguments
- **LinkedIn dominance is real**: 73-80% of consultant client discovery happens there [P3CON-016, P3CON-017]. Any alternative has to either ride alongside LinkedIn (export-target, not replacement) or pick a wedge LinkedIn under-serves (NDA-safe case-study formatting; AI-visibility optimization; framework-library hosting). Frontal attack will fail.
- **Catalant + Toptal already do "vetted consultant marketplace"** and have AI-matching [P3CON-021]. Differentiation must be identity/portfolio first, marketplace second.
- **Confidentiality enforcement is real**. Any product that encourages consultants to "show their work" must build NDA-safe affordances by default; otherwise users will churn after the first legal review.
- **The fractional bubble could deflate**. The 55x LinkedIn-profile growth from 2K to 110K likely includes a significant "wantrepreneur" cohort with no clients. Real revenue is concentrated in a smaller subset. Survivorship/aspirational confound.
- **Consultants are notoriously skeptical of self-serve software**. They sell expertise; they suspect tools that promise expertise.

### D.6 Recommended entry priority

**8/10.**

Rationale (in plain prose): consulting ranks as one of the top three entry-points for this product among the industries surveyed, behind only sales (probably) and design (probably). The case for high priority:
- ~100K+ self-identified fractionals already paying for brand-building, growing 50%+ YoY [P3CON-006]
- NDA constraint is a uniquely durable wedge: no existing product solves "how do I display credibility when I can't show the artifact"
- High willingness to share rich professional data (we want it; they want to be found)
- High LTV ($150-$500/hour effective rates; willing to pay for pipeline tools) [P3CON-018]
- AI-visibility anxiety is a fresh, unfilled vector [P3CON-014]
- B-side mapping is clean (recruiters want vertical x functional x AI-stack expertise)

The case for caution (why not 9 or 10):
- LinkedIn dominance is severe and consultants will not abandon it
- Many consultants (especially big-firm staff) have firm communications restrictions
- Confidentiality cage means heavy product investment in NDA-safe authoring tools before scale

**Suggested initial wedge**: fractional executives + ex-big-firm independents (the ~200-400K-person global cohort). Position not as "LinkedIn replacement" but as "AI-era credibility portfolio that exports to LinkedIn + makes you findable by AI assistants." First feature: a structured NDA-safe case-study format with outcome quantification, framework repositories, and AI-search-engine indexing.

## Cross-Cutting Observations (Consulting-Specific)

1. **Consulting is the leading indicator industry**. What AI did to junior consultants in 2024-2025 is what AI will do to junior analysts in finance, junior associates in law, and junior copywriters in marketing in 2026-2028. Whatever identity product works for consulting professionals will likely generalize.
2. **Prestige signal is decoupling from output capability**. AI lets an ex-MBB indie deliver MBB-grade decks; meanwhile the MBB brand still carries hiring weight at large companies. This decoupling creates an arbitrage that benefits independents who can credibly claim equivalent output.
3. **The deck is dead as a portfolio artifact**. When Lilli/Deckster can produce a polished deck in minutes, "I made this deck" stops being a credible expertise signal. What replaces it: framework, methodology, outcome, named case study with verifiable client reference.
4. **Specialization is being rewarded harder than ever**. AI commoditizes generic frameworks; the 30-40% boutique price advantage requires sharp niching [P3CON-019]. Our product must allow ultra-narrow positioning ("Fractional CMO for B2B SaaS, Series-B, EU healthtech, AI-native product orgs") - not 5 broad categories.

## Research Limitations

- **Reddit blackout**: WebSearch returned no usable r/consulting threads despite multiple targeted queries. Direct subreddit qualitative voice is missing; consultant frustrations are inferred from secondary sources, not heard verbatim. This is a real evidence gap.
- **Paywalled primary sources**: HBR, Bloomberg, and McKinsey direct sources were partially or fully inaccessible via WebFetch (timeouts and 403s). Relied on summary aggregators and trade press; cross-referenced where possible but some quote attribution is second-hand.
- **Survivorship in fractional numbers**: "110K fractional profiles" is *self-identification on LinkedIn*, not active clients. Real working fractionals are likely a much smaller subset; growth rates may overstate market depth.
- **Vendor source bias**: many AI-consulting articles are written by AI-consulting vendors, whose business model depends on the narrative being true. Triangulated where possible (BCG/McKinsey primary numbers, BLS workforce, Toptal/EY survey data) but bias is non-zero.
- **Geographic skew**: nearly all evidence is US-focused (and partly UK/EU through Consultport/Malt). India, Japan, and other large consulting markets are under-represented; the fractional movement may be much weaker outside North America.
- **No consultant interviews**: this is desk research only. No primary qualitative data from a working consultant about their identity-product preferences. Real product validation requires consultant interviews especially in the indie/fractional segment.
- **Date inconsistency in sources**: many "2025" sources are written in mid-2025 looking forward, some "2026" sources are best-fit aggregations. Where claims conflict, I weighted toward primary firm announcements over commentary.
- **Did not estimate revenue concentration**: the report assumes "indies are a real and growing user pool" but does not estimate what fraction earns enough to pay for SaaS tooling. Real product-pricing decisions will need this.

End of report.
