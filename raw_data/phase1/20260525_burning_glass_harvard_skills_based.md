# Burning Glass Institute × Harvard Business School: Skills-Based Hiring — The Long Road from Pronouncements to Practice

Source: https://www.burningglassinstitute.org/research/skills-based-hiring-2024
Also: https://www.hbs.edu/bigs/joseph-fuller-college-degree-gap
Also: https://www.hrdive.com/news/employers-failing-at-skills-based-hiring/707897/
Date: February 14, 2024
Authors: Joseph Fuller (HBS) + Burning Glass Institute team.

## Methodology
- Analysed online job postings 2014-2023.
- 11,300 roles at large firms with at least 1 year of data before/after degree-requirement removal.
- ~400% increase in annual number of roles where employers removed degree requirements over the decade.

## Central finding
- Despite the policy churn, hiring of workers without a BA increased only 3.5 percentage points on average in the affected roles.
- Net effect across the economy: ~97,000 additional non-degree hires out of 77 million yearly hires in 2023 — fewer than 1 in 700.
- Net 0.14 percentage point increase in non-degree hiring share when you average across all affected roles.

## Three employer categories
- Skills-Based Hiring **Leaders** (37%): made real changes, ~20% more non-degree hires.
- **In-Name-Only** (45%): announcements but no actual change in hiring patterns.
- **Backsliders** (18%): initial drop then reinstated/reduced again.

## Outcomes at the Leaders
- Non-degree hires showed 10 percentage points higher retention than degreed peers.
- 25% average salary increase for non-degree workers entering roles that previously required a BA.

## Named "leader" companies
Apple, Cigna, ExxonMobil, General Motors, Koch Industries, Target, Tyson Foods, Walmart. Also government employers (Minnesota, Denver).

## Quotes — Joseph Fuller
- "We found measurable but pretty modest changes, and that some companies have done better than others."
- "Changing your hiring policy is, at best, the end of the beginning."
- Report text: "Our analysis makes clear that successful adoption of skills-based hiring involves more than simply stripping language from job postings"; needs "robust and intentional changes."

## Significance
This is the strongest piece of cold evidence against the "skills/works > credentials" narrative. The signal is loud (announcements) but the action is small (0.14pp). It does NOT, however, refute that *AI* specifically is now hard-required in postings — that's a separate trend with separate evidence.
