# Path — Failure Analysis (2010–2018)
**Citation IDs: V434–V438**

## Overview
- Founded: November 14, 2010
- Founders: Dave Morin (co-founder, former Facebook design lead), Dustin Mierau, Shawn Fanning (Napster co-founder)
- Premise: Intimate social network — limited to 150 "close friends" (Dunbar's number); photo sharing + messaging for real relationships
- Total raised: $66.2–$77M (sources vary; $66.2M confirmed by multiple shutdown reports; Kleiner Perkins, Greylock, Shasta Ventures, Redpoint among investors)
- Acquired: May 28, 2015 by Daum Kakao (Korean internet firm); acquisition price undisclosed
- Shutdown: September 17, 2018

## Timeline
- November 2010: Launch; 1.5M downloads within three weeks; strong early hype
- January 2011: 3 months post-launch; Google offered to buy Path for $100M — Dave Morin declined (shocked observers)
- February 2011: Series B, $8.5M from Kleiner Perkins and Index Ventures
- November 2011: Major redesign; reached 2 million users
- February 2012: Privacy scandal — Path discovered uploading entire user address books to servers without permission; public backlash
- February 2013: FTC fined Path $800,000 for storing data from underage users; required biennial privacy audits for 20 years
- 2013–2014: Declined to raise new VC; lost most Western users; pivoted focus to South Korea/Asia market
- 2014: Company struggling; high user concentration in Indonesia and South Korea
- May 2015: Sold to Daum Kakao; price undisclosed; Dave Morin departed
- September 2018: Platform shut down entirely

## Peak Users / Funding
- Peak valuation: ~$500M (reported during early 2010s peak)
- Peak users: ~15–20M registered users (heavily concentrated in Asia by end), 2M after 2011 redesign, likely 5–10M peak before decline
- Total funding: $66.2M burned through by shutdown

## Multi-Factor Cause of Death

**Product**
- "Intimate social network" concept had fundamental engagement ceiling: if you only share with 150 close friends, you post less, see less, return less
- No viral/public content loop — unlike Twitter/Instagram, content was locked behind the friend circle
- Privacy was the core value proposition, but the 2012 address book scandal was a catastrophic brand contradiction
- Never found a sustainable engagement mechanic that made daily return compelling

**Market**
- Failed to attract users outside the tech crowd — product was conceptually ahead of mainstream behavior
- TAM problem: the people who wanted "intimate" social networking were small; Facebook dominated casual social
- Turned to Korea/Indonesia as secondary markets — but this was a retreat, not a growth strategy

**Team/Capital**
- Rejected Google's $100M offer at 3 months old (perhaps the single costliest decision)
- Dave Morin's vision (luxury, intimate, designed) may have limited product adaptability
- Heavy spending ($66.2M burned) on a consumer product that never monetized well

**Timing**
- Launched exactly as Facebook was reaching dominance; niche positioning alone isn't enough against network-effect incumbents
- Privacy concerns in 2012 were culturally present but not yet the political/regulatory issue they became by 2018 — the scandal came too early to be "validated" and too late to recover

**Capital**
- Without a path to monetization, $66.2M wasn't enough to outlast Facebook
- Post-privacy scandal, new VC refused to fund; no path to IPO or major acquisition at fair value

## Key Lesson for Us
- Turning down $100M at 3 months old is almost never right — founder conviction can become a liability
- Privacy as a brand promise requires absolute perfection in execution; one scandal erases the entire proposition
- "Intimate" / small-circle networking reduces viral sharing loops and weakens growth mechanics
- Never rely on a single geography — pivot to Asia saved the company temporarily but signaled defeat
