# Strava — Cold Start Data (2009–2014)
**Citation IDs: V405–V409**

## Founders
- Mark Gainey and Michael Horvath — met at Harvard as members of the rowing crew in the 1980s
- Original "virtual locker room for athletes" concept dated to 1995 (business plan written then but technology not ready)
- Officially launched Strava in 2009, San Francisco; seed investors: Sigma Partners

## First ~100 Users — Where From
- July 2008 (pre-launch prototype): recruited 20 cyclist friends to test with Garmin GPS devices during Tour de France
- Initial test community was personal cycling friends of founders in San Francisco Bay Area
- "For the better part of 2009 it was just the six of them telling their friends and getting them to sign up, and they had to sign them up because they didn't even have that functionality yet"
- One notable first customer: "David" — still with Strava 12-13 years later (Mark Gainey quote)
- Strategy described as "inch wide, mile deep" — targeting passionate road cyclists ("MAMILs": middle-aged men in lycra)

## First ~1,000 Users — Where From
- SF Bay Area cycling community — leveraged that "people in the venture community are cyclists or runners who understand this audience"
- Segments feature emerged organically from user conversations about iconic climbs during rides
- MVP was a leaderboard tracking efforts across a few Vermont and California cycling segments
- Kitchel conceived of segment leaderboards from an unofficial hill-climb competition from a local group ride in the late 1990s
- Early athletes were "calling in sick to work to go ride their bikes and challenge the segment leaderboard" — Horvath

## Key Product Decisions (First 2 Years)
- Segments: iconic road climbs with leaderboards — created comparative metrics, drove competitive repeat engagement
- Social elements followed naturally: users wanted to see how friends performed on the same segments
- Kudos (lightweight social validation) added after Segments proved engagement
- "Green Machine" — experimental website testing raw activity data before full product launch
- Ruthless feature culling: dozens prototyped, only those sparking delight survived

## Time to 100K and 1M Users
- 2009–2010: tens of thousands (SF cycling niche)
- 2011: launched iOS and Android apps — removed reliance on Garmin GPS units — shifted from web-upload to mobile; pushed users from tens of thousands to millions
- 2012: added running — instantly tripled addressable market
- 2016: 20 million users (self-reported); by 2017 roughly 1M new members every 40 days
- Specific 1M user date: not publicly confirmed; likely 2012–2013 after mobile launch and running expansion

## Initial Monetization Model
- Freemium: free basic tracking, premium subscription ($6/month) for advanced analytics and features
- Premium launched approximately 2010–2011 alongside mobile apps
- No advertising model — relied on subscription from the start

## Invite-Only?
- No formal invite system; grassroots word-of-mouth within cycling community
- Natural network effect: segments only valuable if friends also use the platform
