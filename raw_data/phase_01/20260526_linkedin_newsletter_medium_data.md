# LinkedIn Newsletter & Medium Newsletter Platform Data

**TL;DR (150-250 words):** LinkedIn Newsletter (launched 2021) has grown to a surprising scale: 28 million unique members subscribe to newsletters on the platform, with 500 million professional newsletter subscriptions total (this is a different, larger metric — LinkedIn auto-subscribes your followers when you publish). The average creator has 2,800 subscribers; top newsletters hit 500K+. The platform's structural advantage is that LinkedIn will notify your existing followers about your newsletter, eliminating cold-start problems that plague Substack beginners. The engagement is real: LinkedIn newsletter open rates average around 40%+ vs. email industry standards of ~21%. However, LinkedIn Newsletters are not email lists you own — content lives on LinkedIn, discovery depends on LinkedIn's algorithm, and you cannot export your subscriber data the way you can with Substack or Beehiiv. Medium's newsletter/membership model has lost relevance for professional creators — it peaked around 2018-2020, was partially eclipsed by Substack's launch, and the Medium Partner Program payouts are small and opaque. Two dead platforms worth noting: Meta Bulletin (shuttered 2022) and Twitter/X Revue (shuttered January 2023 when Elon Musk killed it). Their failures reinforced creator anxiety about platform dependency, which is one reason Substack's "you own your list" message resonated so strongly in 2023-2025.

### Why the 500M vs 28M Gap?
LinkedIn auto-subscribes your followers when you publish your first newsletter issue. The 500M "subscriptions" metric includes all these auto-subscriptions. The 28M "unique member subscribers" is the more meaningful number — people who actually follow at least one newsletter by choice.

### Creator Income on LinkedIn
- Top 1% of LinkedIn Creators: $15,000-$250,000+/year through partnerships, consulting, speaking
- LinkedIn does not have a built-in paid subscription model for newsletters (unlike Substack)
- Monetization is indirect: brand deals, consulting inquiries, course sales driven by newsletter audience

### Strategic Assessment
**Advantage:** Enormous built-in professional audience; algorithm amplifies new newsletters to followers; best platform for B2B professional content

**Disadvantage:** You do not own the email list; subscriber data cannot be exported; content is LinkedIn-locked; monetization is indirect only

## Medium Newsletter / Medium Partner Program

### Platform Context
- Medium launched 2012 (Ev Williams, co-founder of Twitter)
- Medium Partner Program launched 2017 — pays writers based on reading time from paying members ($5/month)
- Peak relevance for professional writers: 2017-2020
- Decline: Substack launch (2017) + aggressive platform growth (2020-2021) redirected serious writers

### Current Status (2025-2026)
- No specific 2024-2025 subscriber or revenue data surfaced in research
- Medium payouts remain small and unpredictable for most writers
- Professional creators (especially in tech/marketing/consulting) have largely migrated to Substack or Beehiiv
- Medium retains value as a distribution channel (Google indexing) but not as a newsletter-first platform

**Assessment:** Medium is not a significant player in the 2025 professional newsletter ecosystem. It functions more as a blog platform with some discovery features.

## Dead Platforms: Object Lessons in Platform Risk

### Meta Bulletin (2021-2022)
- Meta launched Bulletin in June 2021, directly competing with Substack
- Featured Malcolm Gladwell, Erin Andrews, and other prominent creators
- Shuttered in early 2022 — Meta quietly ended the program
- Lesson: Even well-resourced platform entrants can fail if the cultural/social context isn't right

### Twitter/X Revue (2021-2023)
- Twitter acquired Revue (newsletter platform) in January 2021
- Promised tight integration with Twitter's social graph as growth mechanism
- Elon Musk shut down Revue in January 2023 shortly after acquiring Twitter
- All Revue newsletters had to migrate; many went to Substack or Beehiiv
- Lesson: Platform dependency risk is real. Creators who had built on Revue lost their infrastructure overnight.

Both closures strengthened the "own your email list" narrative that Substack and Beehiiv have built their marketing around.
