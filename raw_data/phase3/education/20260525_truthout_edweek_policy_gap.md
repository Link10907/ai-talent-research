## The "permission-without-policy" problem (Truthout, 2025)
"Tools are in buildings, use is already underway, and the guidance that would make that use purposeful and protected is not in place. Teachers receive the message that AI is coming and they are expected to embrace it, often without serious discussion of pedagogy, ethics, or structural inequities AI may deepen."

## Mandate vs. voluntary tension
- Some districts MANDATE AI tool use (teachers required to use it whether they want to or not)
- Other districts BAN AI broadly (NYC Public Schools, LA Unified initially)
- Most are in a gray zone: tools accessible, no formal policy
- Result: each teacher individually decides, with no institutional support

## Teacher sentiment is genuinely split
- 19th News: many teachers voluntarily adopting for burnout relief
- Edutopia: published essays from teachers banning AI in their own classrooms in 2025-26 (e.g., Chanea Bond piece)
- EdWeek Jan 2025: "schools' AI policies still not clear to teachers and students"

## Implication
- Education industry sentiment is the most polarized of any white-collar field
- Even within one school, you can find: enthusiastic AI-adopter teachers, resistant traditionalist teachers, and quiet-cheating students — all simultaneously
- This polarization means a "professional identity around AI" product is risky in K-12: half the audience would oppose its premise
- The polarization is much weaker in corporate L&D and ID — those professions have already largely accepted AI as core competency
