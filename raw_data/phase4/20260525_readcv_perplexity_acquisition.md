# Read.cv Acquisition / Shutdown — Source Material

## Primary facts (cross-verified from TechCrunch + multiple sources)
- Founded 2021 by Andy Chung (ex-Facebook, Mozilla, Quip/Salesforce product designer)
- Backed by F7 Ventures and Fanjul Capital (no public funding amount)
- ~3 employees at shutdown
- Acquired by Perplexity announced Jan 17, 2025
- Read.cv stopped operations Jan 17, 2025; data export deadline May 16, 2025
- .cv domains migrated to Hello.cv from Jan 31

## Founder's stated reason (Andy Chung, posts.cv)
"Ultimately the challenges of sustaining a small business were more than we could overcome."

## Perplexity rationale (Aravind Srinivas, TechCrunch)
"The team is incredibly capable in designing and building consumer and social experiences and we look forward to working with them on many new exciting directions!"
[Note: this reads as acqui-hire, not product acquisition. Perplexity has no public plan to revive Read.cv as a product.]

## What Read.cv WAS (from Medium retrospectives + Hack Design)

### From hackdesign.org:
"Read.cv reimagined the professional profile for designers and creatives who want something more thoughtful than LinkedIn but less maintenance-heavy than a custom portfolio site. The platform's work history became a curated timeline, projects got beautiful layouts without code, and profiles felt like they were art-directed rather than algorithmically assembled."

### From patriciaparnet (Medium R.I.P. piece):
- Described as "a show don't tell platform" celebrating creative work
- Featured "digital gardens, independent film, zines, furniture making, self-published music" alongside professional profiles
- Called itself "a calm place for everyone" without commercial pressure
- "Minimalistic and yet so beautiful" but author acknowledges it remained "niche product serving creatives rather than the broader professional market"
- Author quote: "We don't just want another LinkedIn. We want something better."

### From Facundo Matiaude (Medium eulogy):
- Genuine interaction happened organically; new users received real responses to first posts (different from broadcast-style LinkedIn)
- "Super organized layout, a clean focus on content, no clutter or ads all over the place"
- "Niche, meaningful conversations instead of transactional interactions"
- Community embodied "a culture of trust — where quality mattered more than quantity"
- Job postings operated on "mutual respect rather than aggressive recruiting tactics"
- Author theory: "can't have your cake and eat it too" — beloved small-scale product gets acquired by larger company because growth pressures make human-centered platforms unable to remain independent

## Death-mechanism analysis (synthesized)
1. **Niche too narrow to support sustainable revenue**: served creative/designer subset, not the bulk of professionals. With ~3 employees, no path to scale ad/sub revenue at LinkedIn scale.
2. **Quality-vs-growth contradiction**: the very thing users loved (curated, calm, no aggressive recruiting) was structurally incompatible with the network effects/monetization needed to survive.
3. **Acqui-hire is signal of failure to find PMF for independent business**, not validation. Perplexity is taking the team, not the product. Existing community got dispersed.
4. **Funding mismatch**: small angel/seed-stage funding with niche audience didn't generate VC-scale outcomes, but neither did it bootstrap to profitability.
