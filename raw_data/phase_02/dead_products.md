# Dead Products: Cause-of-Death Narratives
**Collected:** 20260526
**Citation IDs:** V100–V106

---

## TL;DR (150-250 words)
Five cautionary tales in the professional identity / networking space reveal recurring failure patterns: (1) professional networks live or die by network effects, and there is no "lite" version of overcoming LinkedIn's gravity; (2) well-funded product pivots (Polywork's switch from LinkedIn-competitor to personal-site-builder) typically destroy early-adopter loyalty without gaining new users; (3) acqui-hires (Read.cv → Perplexity) are the "soft exit" that leaves communities orphaned; (4) AI-networking tools (Lunchclub) can generate engagement but not retention when the core value proposition requires the platform to work perfectly every time; (5) over-funded social networking with a strong vision (Path's privacy-first social graph) collapses when the unit economics never close. The through-line: professional identity products attract passionate early adopters who are not enough to sustain a VC-scale business. Contra is the only survivor in this cohort, having repositioned from "professional identity display" toward "commission-free freelance marketplace" — a clearer value proposition with real revenue mechanics.

## 1. Read.cv (Shut down January 2025, Perplexity acqui-hire)

### Product
Minimalist professional profile platform. Created beautiful, design-forward "work CVs" — more curated than LinkedIn, less noisy. Added Posts.cv (micro-blogging), Sites feature (personal websites on .cv domains), and job board.

### Founding
2021, Andy Chung (ex-Facebook, Mozilla, Quip). West Berkeley, CA. Team: 3 people at time of acquisition.

### What Went Wrong

**Product problem:** Read.cv was genuinely loved by design-conscious tech workers, but love didn't convert to money. The Sites feature (.cv custom domains) was the clearest monetization vector — but it launched late and required Read.cv to compete with Squarespace, Carrd, and Webflow.

**Market problem:** Professional networks require bilateral network effects. For Read.cv to be valuable for recruiting, both candidates AND recruiters had to be there. Recruiters never showed up in meaningful numbers.

**Team/capital problem:** Three-person team. No disclosed funding raised publicly. The team was extraordinarily lean — beautiful product, but no margin for error on growth.

**The exit:** Perplexity acquired the team to work on consumer/social products within its AI search product. The platform wound down with a May 2025 data export deadline. Users could download their CV as a Next.js project for self-hosting.

### Founder Quote
Andy Chung: "incredibly bittersweet" — on the acquisition.

### Community Reaction (HN)
"Yet again, let down by a centralised platform." Users moved to Reactive Resume (open-source self-hosted), personal sites, or LinkedIn by default.

### Lesson
- Profile-display products need a clear revenue path independent of "network effects eventually"
- The acqui-hire exit is real — small teams building beloved professional tools should plan for it
- Users correctly interpreted this as platform risk: centralized identity is fragile

## 2. Polywork (Shut down January 31, 2025)

### Product
Professional network that allowed multi-dimensional profiles — not just job titles but skills, side projects, hobbies. Golden Kitty Award winner on Product Hunt (2022). Later pivoted to AI-powered personal website builder.

### Funding
$13M raised (confirmed). Backers: Stripe co-founders John and Patrick Collison, Reddit co-founder Alexis Ohanian, Elad Gil. Total often cited as $44.5M raised (including undisclosed rounds). One source cites $1.45B as peak valuation discussion — likely aspirational/media-reported, not a confirmed round at that valuation.

### Timeline
- 2021: Launch
- 2022: Product Hunt Golden Kitty Award
- Late 2023: Pivot to AI website builder
- January 2025: Shutdown announced by founder Peter Johnston
- January 31, 2025: Platform closed

### Founder Statement (X, @multiplay3r, January 2025)
Peter Johnston posted: "after 3.5 years, I made the call to shut @Polywork down and return the remaining capital to investors. Working on Polywork, especially in the first 18 months was the highlight of my career."

Key quotes:
- "Building consumer social is like doing a startup on Level 900 Difficulty Mode, and while that definitely rang true, it was a privilege to get to work on solving the problem of professional identity and discovery."
- "Unfortunately, we were unable to sustain our growth and no longer saw a viable path to Polywork becoming a gigantic business — and that was what we signed up for."
- "I still really believe the world needs a new standard in professional identity and discovery, and so I am hopeful that one day, another team takes a crack at this problem again."

### What Went Wrong

**Product problem:** Polywork's multi-identity promise was real, but the product couldn't nail what professionals actually wanted from a second network. Most professionals don't want another profile to maintain — they want their *existing* profile to be better.

**Market problem:** Regional network effects are brutal. If your country/city isn't represented, the platform feels empty. Polywork had strong US early-adopter traction but couldn't replicate it globally or even outside SF/NYC.

**Pivot problem:** The 2023 pivot from "LinkedIn alternative" to "AI website builder" was strategically incoherent — these are entirely different user jobs. Website builders have established commoditized players (Squarespace, Carrd, Framer, Notion). A startup with stalled growth switching to fight them needed a differentiated reason to switch. An AI-generated personal site from your LinkedIn data is a compelling feature, not a business.

**Capital problem:** Returning capital to investors suggests the company had funds remaining — this is a *chosen* shutdown, not a forced one. Johnston read the market signal: no path to becoming "a gigantic business."

### Community Reaction
HN: "They misread the correct value proposition to compete." "Started as LinkedIn competitor but ended up becoming a website builder." "Too many page builders already and chasing profit for VCs has killed another good idea."

### Lessons
- Network effects can't be manufactured with design or features alone
- A profile platform is not a website builder; pivoting between them destroys the core value prop
- "LinkedIn but better" is a necessary but insufficient differentiator
- Star investor logos (Collisons, Ohanian) don't substitute for PMF

## 3. Lunchclub (Status: Zombie / Severely Reduced Operations)

### Product
AI-powered networking tool that matched professionals for 1:1 video calls. Founded 2018. Backed by Andreessen Horowitz. The premise: instead of browsing LinkedIn, you get algorithmically matched with one relevant professional per week.

### Funding
Raised ~$4M seed (a16z-led). Peak users: ~1M+ claimed users across tech, startup, VC community.

### Current Status (2025-2026)
Based on search results: Lunchclub.com still live but activity levels appear severely reduced. Users report account deactivations, support non-responsive. No formal shutdown announcement found. Classic "zombie" state — lights on, no one building.

### What Went Wrong (Observable Signals)

**Engagement retention problem:** The product worked *once* — the first few matches were novel and high-value. But weekly meetings become a scheduling burden. The 1:1 video call format has high coordination overhead.

**Dependency problem:** The value depended entirely on the quality of matches. As the user base stagnated, match quality declined. A smaller, inactive user pool means worse matches, which accelerates user churn.

**Business model problem:** Matching quality as a service is hard to monetize. Enterprise plans (matching your team members) were attempted, but the core consumer product didn't convert to B2B easily.

**COVID tailwind / hangover:** Lunchclub peaked during COVID remote work era when professionals craved human connection. As hybrid work returned, the urgency of AI-powered networking dropped.

### Lesson
- AI matching for human connection is a strong premise but has severe engagement cliffs after the novelty wears off
- Scheduling overhead kills retention
- Platforms built on COVID-era behavior change need a plan for the return to normal

## 4. Path (Social Network, Founded 2010, shut down 2018)

### Product
Dave Morin's "intimate social network" — maximum 150 friends, privacy-first, beautiful mobile design. Backed by Max Levchin, Marc Andreessen, Greylock. Raised ~$55M. Focused on close relationships vs. Facebook's broadcast model.

### What Went Wrong

**Market problem:** Facebook and Instagram owned social networking attention. Path's privacy-first positioning appealed to a small audience of early adopters but couldn't compete for mainstream attention.

**Revenue problem:** Path tried B2B enterprise (Path for Work) and premium features but found no scalable revenue model. Consumer social apps with privacy as a core feature are structurally difficult to monetize (no ads, no data selling).

**Regulatory incident:** Path was fined $800K by FTC in 2013 for collecting contact data from minors without parental consent — significant trust damage for a privacy-positioned product.

**Timing:** Path launched when mobile social was still establishing norms. It was too early for privacy-first positioning (2010-2012), and by 2015-2016 when privacy concerns spiked culturally, it was too late.

**Exit:** Sold to Kakao (Korean messaging platform) in 2015, shut down 2018.

### Lesson for Professional Identity Products
- "Better design" + "more privacy" is not a business model
- The intimate network thesis (Dunbar's number cap at 150) creates a ceiling on network effect growth
- Consumer social apps without an ad model need a clear alternative revenue source

## 5. Contra (Status: Alive, Repositioned)

### Product
Started as a professional identity/portfolio platform for independents — beautiful profiles, commission-free project work. Now primarily a commission-free freelance marketplace. $45M raised (New Enterprise Associates). 1M+ professionals.

### 2024-2025 Status
- Still operating, 20% user growth Q1 2025
- Launched "Indy AI" — AI tool for freelancers to find work
- Repositioned from "professional identity display" → "commission-free freelance marketplace + tools"
- Revenue model: freemium + premium features (not commission on project fees)

### Why It Survived (While Others Didn't)
Contra pivoted *into* a clearer economic value proposition. While Polywork went from network → website builder (two commoditized spaces), Contra went from identity display → commission-free marketplace (one clear competitive advantage against Upwork's 20% take rate).

### Current Identity Play
Contra's profile pages are still one of its selling points — clean, portfolio-forward. But they're positioned as "how clients find and hire you" not "how you manage your professional identity." The purpose is transactional, not expressive.

### Lesson
- The only professional identity platforms that survive long-term are the ones with a transactional layer (GitHub for hiring, Behance for client work, Wellfound for job applications)
- Pure identity display without a clear transactional purpose struggles to monetize

## Synthesis: Why Professional Identity Platforms Keep Dying

| Factor | Read.cv | Polywork | Lunchclub | Path | Contra |
|--------|---------|----------|-----------|------|--------|
| Network effect problem | High | High | Medium | High | Medium |
| Revenue model unclear | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes | Solved (marketplace) |
| Pivot destroyed core value | No | Yes | No | No | No (doubled down) |
| LinkedIn as default competitor | Yes | Yes | No | No | Partial |
| Acqui-hire/acquisition exit | Yes | No | No | Yes | No |
| Status (2026) | Dead | Dead | Zombie | Dead | Alive |

**Key meta-lesson:** The successful players in professional identity are those with either (a) LinkedIn's network effect moat, (b) a specific transactional purpose (GitHub = hiring signal for devs; Behance = client discovery for designers), or (c) owned audiences the professional built themselves (newsletter, personal site). Platforms trying to be "a better LinkedIn profile for everyone" without a transactional hook consistently fail.
