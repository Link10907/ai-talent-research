## TL;DR (180 words)
Forty-two public-figure professionals across six archetypes reveal that LinkedIn functions as the "mandatory professional record" for almost everyone — but it is almost never where notable people *publish first* or consider their *real home*. For tech founders and indie hackers, X is the primary living room (first publication, highest engagement, most authentic voice). Newsletter writers use Substack as their primary identity. Senior engineers maintain rich personal sites as their "real portfolio" alongside GitHub as skills proof. Designers split between Behance/Dribbble (client acquisition) and personal sites (taste expression). Consultants and solopreneurs use LinkedIn as front door but newsletter as relationship deepener. The most consistent finding: people with strong professional identities maintain 2-3 platforms *actively* and treat LinkedIn as a passive record or amplification channel, not as the place they think. "Front door" (where strangers find them) and "living room" (where they publish first) are almost always different platforms. This bifurcation is the key user insight for any professional identity product.

## Patterns Emerging from Sample

### Pattern 1: The "Mandated LinkedIn + Real Home Elsewhere" Split
Almost every tech professional maintains LinkedIn as a "mandatory credential store" but does their real publishing elsewhere. The most active self-presenters (indie hackers, newsletter writers, public engineers) treat LinkedIn as an amplification channel, not a creation channel.
- **Exemplified by:** Pieter Levels (X is home), Lenny Rachitsky (Substack is home), Patrick Collison (personal site is home)

### Pattern 2: The Personal Site as Taste Signal
In tech/design, having a well-crafted personal site signals taste and craft. Not having one (for a senior engineer or designer) is a neutral signal. Having a *bad* one is mildly negative. Having a *great* one is a strong positive signal.
- **Exemplified by:** Patrick Collison (patrickcollison.com as curated thinking), Linus Lee (thesephist.com as project portfolio), Tom Critchlow (tomcritchlow.com as consulting homepage)

### Pattern 3: The Dual-Audience Problem
Most professionals have two audiences: (1) peers/collaborators, (2) clients/employers. The same profile rarely works for both. Engineers write for peers on GitHub; write for employers on LinkedIn. Designers show process on Behance; show personality on Dribbble. This creates maintenance burden.

### Pattern 4: The Creator Escape from LinkedIn
The "Build in Public" cohort (Pieter Levels, Marc Lou, Ben Tossell, Visakan) has essentially escaped LinkedIn entirely. They use X as their primary professional identity — and their identity IS their X account. Follower counts and content archives ARE their CV.

### Pattern 5: The Consultant's LinkedIn Dependency
Consultants (April Dunford, Wes Kao, Tom Critchlow) tend to have the richest multi-platform presence — LinkedIn for inbound leads, newsletter for relationship deepening, personal site for authority signaling. LinkedIn is more important here than for pure-creators because consultants need to reach buyers who are on LinkedIn.

### Pattern 6: The Designer's Portfolio Necessity
Designers in all categories (UX, graphic, motion) are unique in that they *must* show work, not just describe it. This creates a mandatory portfolio layer (Behance, Dribbble, Cargo, Framer site) that other archetypes can skip.

### Pattern 7: The Mid-Level Professional's Frozen LinkedIn
The "silent majority" — Director-level professionals at real companies — treat LinkedIn as frozen credentialing. Profile updated when changing jobs, otherwise dormant. They have no "living room" because they're not publishers. Their "front door" and their "only door" are the same: a mostly-static LinkedIn profile. **This is the underserved market.**
